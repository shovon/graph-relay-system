# The secp256k1 signature profile

A profile is a promise between strangers: pin a scheme concretely enough that two implementations, written by people who never coordinated, still produce the same signatures and reconstruct the same identities. Several documents beneath this one leave the signature scheme abstract on purpose. The [signed hash chain](../signed-hash-chain/signed-hash-chain.md) needs only that "you can verify a signature given a public key"; recovering a party's identity from a signature alone — the capability its [key-omitted form](../signed-hash-chain/without-embedded-keys/without-embedded-keys.md) leaves to the scheme rather than requiring — needs more: a $\text{Recover}$ operation, with ECDSA with public-key recovery named as the canonical family, but stopping short of a curve. The overlay that commits the out-edge into each link inherits exactly that abstraction, in both its [key-carrying](../overlay/path-emergence/out-edge-commitment.md) and [recovered-identity](../overlay/path-emergence/recovered-node-identity.md) forms. This document closes the gap for all of them with one concrete instance: the signature scheme is **ECDSA over secp256k1**, hashing with **SHA-256**.

Everything those documents claim holds over any scheme meeting their abstract requirement; this one just picks the instance — so that independent implementations, including third-party ones that conform to this page without absorbing the overlay's routing semantics, produce signatures and node IDs that actually interoperate. What is committed here is the curve, the algorithm, and the identity encoding; the on-wire byte framing of a signature is the business of the [encoding binding](./recoverable-ecdsa-signature.md) this points to. Fix both and two strangers can agree byte-for-byte.

## 1. Why secp256k1

The deciding property is **recovery** — because this overlay leans on it as a first-class operation, not an afterthought. The [recovered-node-identity](../overlay/path-emergence/recovered-node-identity.md) form _defines_ a node by the key that verification reconstructs from a signature; $\text{Recover}$ is on the hot path of establishing who signed a hop. secp256k1 is the curve where that operation is mature, ubiquitous, and battle-tested: it is Bitcoin's and Ethereum's curve, `ecrecover` is a primitive the entire Ethereum ecosystem depends on, and the well-audited libraries in that world (noble-secp256k1, libsecp256k1, and their kin) expose recovery — and the recovery id — as a first-class call rather than something you reconstruct by hand.

The honest cost, stated plainly, is the mirror image of that ubiquity: secp256k1 is **not platform-native in the browser**. The WebCrypto API (`SubtleCrypto`) implements P-256, not secp256k1, and most operating-system keystores and secure enclaves are the same. For an overlay that expects nodes in the browser — this is a WebSocket graph — that means two concessions. A node must **bundle a curve implementation** of its own rather than call a built-in primitive (small, audited pure-JS/WASM libraries exist, so this is weight, not danger), and it generally **cannot hold its private key in hardware-backed, non-exportable storage** the way a P-256 key could. We buy first-class recovery at the price of platform-native signing.

That trade is worth making here precisely because the alternatives don't actually escape the cost:

- **P-256** (`secp256r1` / `prime256v1`) is platform-native and recovery-capable *in principle*, but every platform crypto API — `SubtleCrypto`, keystores, enclaves — **withholds the recover operation**: they sign and verify against a supplied key and expose neither a recover call nor the recovery id. So the recovery form would have to supply recovery itself anyway. You pay the "ship EC arithmetic" cost regardless, and you get it on a curve whose recovery tooling is far less exercised than secp256k1's.
- **Ed25519** is platform-available and pleasant, but offers no public-key recovery at all, which forecloses the [recovered-identity](../overlay/path-emergence/recovered-node-identity.md) form outright.

Given that the recovery form is a design goal and no curve lets a browser node recover from the platform, secp256k1 is the choice that puts the operation we depend on on its most trodden path.

## 2. What is committed

- **Curve.** secp256k1 (SEC 2). Call its group order $n$.
- **Signature.** ECDSA, producing a pair $(r, s)$ — the chain's $\sigma_i$.
- **Hash.** SHA-256, matching the curve's ~128-bit security level, applied wherever the chain calls for a hash — both the running hash $h_i$ and the digest each $\sigma_i$ signs. This profile commits the hash *function*, and that it is a **single** SHA-256 (not a double hash); it does not commit *what* is hashed. The tuple that forms a signed input is the [chain](../signed-hash-chain/signed-hash-chain.md)'s to define and the [length-prefixed encoding](../encodings/length-prefixed-encoding.md)'s to lay out in bytes — for the overlay's trail that is the running-hash form $(i, h_{i-1}, m_i)$ fixed by [trail-wire-format](../overlay/path-emergence/trail-wire-format.md) — and stating it here would only duplicate, and risk contradicting, the documents that own it.
- **Self-certifying ID.** A node's ID $p_i$ is its secp256k1 public key in **compressed SEC1 point form** — 33 octets, a parity byte followed by the $x$-coordinate. This is what the chain's abstract "the self-certifying ID is the public key, or a derivation thereof" resolves to here: it is what an out-edge commitment is signed by, and, in the recovery form, what verification reconstructs.

The concrete octet layout of $(r, s)$ — fixed-width $r \Vert s$ versus DER, and whether a recovery id rides alongside — is a wire-encoding choice; committing to one is the business of an encoding binding, in the spirit of the chain's [length-prefixed encoding](../encodings/length-prefixed-encoding.md). The overlay makes that commitment in [the recoverable signature encoding](./recoverable-ecdsa-signature.md), which fixes Bitcoin's 65-byte compact form for the recovery variant and the bare 64-byte pair for the base one. This document fixes the curve and algorithm, not the byte framing.

## 3. Malleability: commit to low-$s$

ECDSA is malleable: for a valid $(r, s)$, the pair $(r,\, n - s)$ also verifies. Under the recovery form this is sharpest, because the recovery id — the parity bit that names the nonce point — interacts with $s$. Hold the recovery id fixed and flip $s$, and recovery lands on a **different key**: an unowned phantom node nobody holds the private key for. (Flip the parity bit *along with* $s$ and you recover the original key again; the danger is precisely that an attacker need not.) Recovered-node-identity flagged this as an identity concern, sharpest at the terminal hop, where nothing chains over the last signature to pin it down.

Committing to secp256k1 therefore also commits to a canonical form: **low-$s$ normalization**, $s \le n/2$. A signature carrying the high $s$ is non-canonical and rejected, whatever its recovery id, so every hop maps to exactly one signature and exactly one recovered node. This is the same normalization Ethereum made consensus-critical in EIP-2, and for the same reason — removing the second signature so the recovery id cannot be played against $s$.

## 4. Recovery is native to the ecosystem — but signing is your job too

Choosing secp256k1 flips the platform story relative to P-256, in both directions.

The good direction is recovery. SEC 1's recovery procedure is generic over prime-field curves, and on secp256k1 it is not a paper capability: the recovery id in $\{0,1,2,3\}$ together with $(r, s)$ and the message recovers the key, and the ubiquitous libraries expose exactly that call. The [recovery form](../overlay/path-emergence/recovered-node-identity.md) is well-served — either carry the one-to-two-bit recovery id beside each signature so the recovered key is unambiguous, or recover-and-match against a candidate set, using tooling that already does this.

The cost direction is that there is **no platform signer to lean on**. Because `SubtleCrypto` and the keystores don't speak secp256k1, *both* companion forms bundle a curve implementation — even the [base form](../overlay/path-emergence/out-edge-commitment.md), where verification is just "verify $\sigma_i$ against $p_i$," runs against a shipped library rather than a built-in. There is no friction-free path here the way P-256's base form had one. That is the honest cost of choosing the recovery-native curve over the platform-native one, and it is part of why the base and recovery forms are still worth keeping as separate options — they differ in what they carry on the wire, even though both now carry a curve implementation in the binary.

## 5. A note on nonces

ECDSA's one catastrophic failure mode is the per-signature nonce $k$: reuse it across two signatures, or draw it with detectable bias, and the private key falls out. On P-256 one could sometimes push this onto a platform signer that owns $k$; here that escape hatch is gone. Because the overlay ships its own secp256k1 implementation and **signs for itself in every form**, deriving $k$ deterministically per **RFC 6979** is not a conditional nicety but the standing expectation — never trust an ambient RNG for it. This is a hazard of any ECDSA deployment, not of secp256k1 in particular, but committing to ECDSA-that-we-run-ourselves is committing to owning it.
